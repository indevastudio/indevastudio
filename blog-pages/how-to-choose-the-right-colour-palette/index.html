colour theory

how to choose the right colour palette for your home

july 2024 · 8 min read
colour decisions are permanent until they're not — and undoing them is expensive. this is our systematic approach to building a palette that works across every room, in every light.

colour selection is the design decision that clients most often make on instinct and most often regret. the instinct is understandable — colour is the most emotionally immediate quality of a space. we respond to it faster than we respond to proportion, texture, or light. but emotional immediacy is not the same as considered judgement. a colour that looks beautiful on a small swatch in a paint store, or on a phone screen, or in a photograph of another person's home, may look entirely different on 40 square metres of wall in your specific light, with your specific flooring, against your specific furniture.

understand your light first

before any colour decision is made, the light conditions of every space must be understood. north-facing rooms in india receive consistent, cool, indirect light — colours read true in this light, but warm tones can feel cold. south-facing rooms receive intense, warm, direct light for much of the day — colours that look warm and rich in a sample can look washed out and harsh in direct midday sun. east-facing rooms have warm morning light that shifts to neutral by afternoon. west-facing rooms glow warmly in the evening but can feel dark and cool in the morning. the same colour behaves differently in each of these conditions.

"test every colour in the actual space, in the actual light, at three different times of day, before committing. everything else is guesswork."

building a coherent palette

a residential colour palette should be coherent — meaning that the colours used throughout the home should relate to each other in a way that creates a sense of progression and harmony rather than disconnection. this doesn't mean every room should be the same colour. it means the palette should be built from a consistent set of undertones. a home where every room has been painted a different colour chosen in isolation — one room terracotta, the next sage green, the next slate blue — will feel fragmented and restless, regardless of the individual quality of each choice.

the testing process

our testing process is simple and non-negotiable: we paint a sample of at least 60x60cm on the actual wall of the actual room. we observe it at three times of day: morning, midday, and evening artificial light. we observe it against the actual flooring and against swatches of the furniture fabrics. we live with it for three days before making a decision. this process eliminates the majority of colour regret. a swatch in a fan deck, or a photograph on a phone, is not an adequate basis for a decision that will cost thousands of rupees to reverse.
