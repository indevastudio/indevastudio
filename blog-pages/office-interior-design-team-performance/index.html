why your office interior design affects your team's performance

november 2024 · 7 min read
the workspace is not neutral. how it looks, feels, and functions has a measurable impact on the people inside it. here's the design evidence.

the relationship between physical environment and cognitive performance is one of the most consistently replicated findings in environmental psychology. people think better, work more effectively, collaborate more productively, and report higher job satisfaction when their physical environment supports the work they are doing. this is not speculative. it is documented across decades of research in dozens of industries. and yet the vast majority of commercial office interiors in india are designed primarily for cost efficiency and photographic appeal — two criteria that have almost nothing to do with the performance of the people who work in them.

the evidence

a study published in the journal of environmental psychology found that workers in offices with natural light reported 84% fewer headaches and eyestrain symptoms and 18% more productivity than those in windowless environments. research by the world green building council found that improved ventilation and air quality in office environments produced a 14% improvement in cognitive function scores. a study by harvard's t.h. chan school of public health found that workers in well-ventilated offices with low pollutant levels scored 101% higher on cognitive function tests than those in conventional air-conditioned environments.

"an office that costs 20% more to design well will produce returns through team performance that dwarf the initial investment within the first year."

what good office design actually requires

effective office design addresses four distinct functional requirements. the first is focus work — environments that allow sustained concentration without acoustic or visual distraction. this typically requires enclosed or semi-enclosed spaces, acoustic treatment, and controlled visual stimulus. the second is collaborative work — environments that facilitate discussion, ideation, and communication between team members. the third is restoration — spaces where people can genuinely recover from cognitive load during the working day. the fourth is social connection — environments that support the informal relationships that are the foundation of organisational culture.

the roi of good office design

the financial case for investing in office design is straightforward: staff cost is, in virtually every knowledge-economy business, the largest single operating expense. a team of forty people earning an average of ₹12 lakhs per year represents a ₹4.8 crore annual payroll investment. a 10% improvement in the effectiveness of that team — a conservative figure, well within the range documented in research — is worth ₹48 lakhs per year. the design investment required to produce that improvement is typically a fraction of that number.
