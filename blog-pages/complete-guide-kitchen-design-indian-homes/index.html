kitchen design

the complete guide to kitchen design in indian homes

january 2025 · 10 min read
the indian kitchen is unlike any other. it cooks hard, lives hard, and needs to look good doing it. here's how to design one that truly works.

the indian kitchen is one of the most demanding functional environments in residential design. it is used more intensively, for longer periods, with more heat, oil, moisture, and aromatic residue than almost any kitchen in the world. it typically serves a household that cooks three full meals daily, produces significant food preparation waste, and requires storage for a complex array of spices, grains, pulses, and equipment. it must do all of this while looking considered, because the kitchen has increasingly become a social and display space rather than a purely functional one. designing it well requires both technical rigour and aesthetic intelligence.

the functional brief: what the indian kitchen actually needs

before any aesthetic decision is made, the functional requirements of the indian kitchen must be established. these include: adequate ventilation for high-heat cooking with strong exhaust capacity; storage for a substantially larger quantity of dry goods than a western kitchen; a wet prep area that can handle intensive vegetable cleaning and cutting; surfaces that can withstand daily exposure to turmeric, tamarind, and hot oil; and a layout that allows two people to work simultaneously without collision. the last point is consistently underestimated — most indian households involve multiple people in daily cooking, and a kitchen designed for single-occupancy use creates constant friction.

"the indian kitchen is not a showroom. it is a working environment. design it first for how it will be used, not for how it will photograph."

layout: the working triangle and beyond

the classic kitchen working triangle — the relationship between hob, sink, and refrigerator — remains valid as a starting point, but requires adaptation for indian cooking patterns. the hob is used more intensively and for longer periods, which means proximity to ventilation and adequate clearance around it is non-negotiable. the prep area needs to be larger than in a western kitchen, because the volume of fresh vegetable preparation is significantly higher. and the relationship between the cooking zone and the serving zone matters more — the distance between hob and dining table is a daily friction point in kitchens where it has not been considered.

material selection: what survives the indian kitchen

counter surfaces in indian kitchens must withstand heat, acid, oil, and staining on a daily basis. granite remains the most functional and durable choice for this reason — it is heat-resistant, acid-resistant when sealed, and virtually indestructible under normal use. engineered quartz performs well in lower-intensity cooking environments but can discolour under sustained high heat. marble, while beautiful, is entirely unsuitable for serious cooking surfaces — it is porous, acid-sensitive, and will stain permanently. cabinet finishes must resist moisture and oil — high-pressure laminate and acrylic remain the most practical choices. lacquered finishes look beautiful initially but require careful maintenance to survive indian cooking conditions long-term.

storage: designing for the indian pantry

the storage requirements of an indian kitchen are substantially greater than those of a western kitchen of equivalent size. a typical indian household stores twenty to forty varieties of spice, five to ten varieties of dal, multiple types of rice and flour, and an array of cooking equipment that includes pressure cookers, woks, tawas, and kadais in multiple sizes. this requires a storage strategy that provides both visibility — so ingredients can be located quickly during cooking — and organisation — so the process of restocking and cooking preparation is efficient. pull-out drawers in base units consistently outperform fixed shelves for spice and grain storage. overhead units should be deep enough to store large vessels but not so deep that the back becomes inaccessible.
