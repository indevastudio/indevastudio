the 8 most expensive interior design mistakes indian homeowners make

march 2024 · 9 min read
most design mistakes are not made out of ignorance — they're made out of impatience. we document the eight decisions that cost clients the most, and how to avoid each one.

in fourteen years of practice, we have been called in to rescue a significant number of projects that have gone wrong. sometimes the failure is technical — a structural issue, a material failure, a contractor dispute. more often, it is a design failure that has manifested as a financial one: decisions made early in the project that cannot be undone without significant cost. these are the eight mistakes we see most frequently, and that cost the most to correct.

mistake 1: starting with furniture, not with space

the most common and most costly mistake in residential design is making furniture purchases before the spatial planning is resolved. a client buys a sofa they love, then discovers it is six inches too wide for the wall they intended it for. or they commission a dining table before the room dimensions have been fixed, and it arrives too large for the space. furniture purchased before planning is complete will always be wrong for some reason. the cost of the furniture is lost. the cost of replacing it is added. the correct sequence is always: plan first, specify second, purchase third.

mistake 2: underspecifying the brief

a brief that does not specify storage requirements, workflow patterns, and non-negotiable functional needs produces a design that looks good but doesn't work. the most common example is a kitchen designed without adequate understanding of how many people cook simultaneously, what equipment is used daily, and how much storage is genuinely required. the result is a beautiful kitchen that is functionally inadequate — and the cost of retrofitting storage, relocating equipment, or reconfiguring the layout is substantial.

"most expensive mistakes are made in the first two weeks of a project. the decisions that seem minor early on become the constraints that are expensive to reverse later."

mistakes 3–8: the compounding errors

the remaining six mistakes are: choosing a contractor on price rather than capability (the cheapest contractor is almost always the most expensive, when remediation costs are included); approving finishes from small samples rather than in-situ (colours and materials always look different at scale); ignoring acoustic design (retrofitting acoustic treatment after construction is both difficult and expensive); choosing fashion over durability in material specification (a finish that looks exceptional on day one but degrades in three years will cost more to replace than a more durable alternative would have cost to specify); not planning for integrated technology (running cables and conduits after walls are finished is extremely expensive); and treating lighting as a purchasing decision rather than a design one (lighting specified from a catalogue rather than designed from first principles will require replacement to correct).
