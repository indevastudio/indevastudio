open plan living: when it works and when it doesn't

may 2024 · 7 min read
open plan became the default modern layout. but defaulting to it without interrogating the brief produces spaces that are neither private enough nor social enough. here's how to decide.

the open-plan living space — a single uninterrupted zone combining living, dining, and kitchen — became the dominant residential layout of the 2000s and 2010s. it was championed by architects and developers for its spatial generosity, its photographic appeal, and its sense of contemporary openness. it was adopted almost universally, across apartment types, family sizes, and lifestyle patterns, with very little interrogation of whether it was actually appropriate for the specific households it was being designed for.

the case for open plan

open plan genuinely works in specific conditions. it works when the household is small — two people or a couple with young children — and social patterns are compatible: when the adults want to be in proximity when cooking, when the living area is genuinely used for entertaining and benefitting from connection to the kitchen, and when acoustic privacy is not a significant need. it also works when the footprint is genuinely generous — open plan in a 600 square foot apartment produces a single large space that serves no function particularly well. open plan in a 1200 square foot apartment can produce a genuinely luxurious sense of spatial continuity.

"open plan is not a style decision. it is a lifestyle decision. make it after interrogating how you actually live."

when open plan fails

open plan fails in several common situations. it fails when one or more members of the household works from home — the acoustic and visual distraction of an open living space is incompatible with sustained concentration work. it fails in households with children of different ages — a teenager needs acoustic separation from a toddler; a parent needs visual separation from both at certain times of day. it fails in households with different sleeping patterns — one partner's early morning in the kitchen is incompatible with another's late sleep in an adjacent open bedroom zone. and it fails when the cooking style is intensive — indian cooking produces significant heat, moisture, and aroma that, in a truly open plan space, permeates the living and sleeping zones.

the hybrid approach

the most effective layouts for most indian households are hybrid — not fully open and not fully closed, but a considered arrangement of spaces that can be visually connected or acoustically separated depending on need. a kitchen with a large servery opening into the dining space, rather than a fully open kitchen, provides the social connection while containing the cooking environment. a living room that is visually open to the entry but separated from the bedroom zone by a partial-height screen or a study alcove provides privacy without enclosure. these hybrid solutions require more careful spatial thinking than either pure open plan or conventional compartmented layouts — and they produce significantly better results.
