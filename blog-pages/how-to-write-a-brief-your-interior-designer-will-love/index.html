how to write a brief your interior designer will actually love

january 2024 · 7 min read
a good brief is the most underrated tool a client has. it saves time, prevents misalignment, and produces dramatically better design outcomes. here's exactly how to write one.

the brief is the most important document in a design project. not the drawings. not the specification. not the contract. the brief — the document that establishes what the project must achieve, for whom, and under what constraints. a good brief produces good design. a poor brief produces design that may be technically accomplished but is wrong for the people who will inhabit it. and a missing brief — which is the most common situation — produces a project that is resolved by assumption rather than by knowledge.

what your brief should contain

a good design brief contains six categories of information. household composition: who lives or works in the space, their ages, their routines, their relationships to each other and to the space. functional requirements: what every space in the project must support — not aspirationally, but specifically. non-negotiables: what cannot be moved, what must be preserved, what must be provided regardless of cost. aesthetic direction: not a list of styles, but a description of how you want the space to feel — the words you would use to describe it to a friend after they'd experienced it. budget: a real number, with a real commitment, verified against what you know comparable projects have cost. timeline: any fixed constraints — a date by which you must be in the space, a event that the project must be complete by.

"the brief is a design act. the client who writes a good one has already made the most important design decisions in the project."

how to describe aesthetic direction without using style words

the hardest part of a brief for most clients is the aesthetic description. style words — modern, contemporary, classic, luxurious — are almost meaningless because they mean different things to different people. the most useful way to describe aesthetic direction is through experience: how do you want to feel when you walk into the room in the morning? what adjectives would a guest use to describe it? what feeling do you most want the space to produce? if you can describe the experience rather than the style, the designer can interpret it in a way that is specific to your space rather than generic.

what makes a brief genuinely useful to a designer

from a designer's perspective, the most valuable information in a brief is not the aesthetic direction — that can be developed collaboratively. the most valuable information is the friction: what doesn't work in your current space? where do you argue with your partner about the way things are organised? what are you embarrassed about when guests come? what is the thing you've wanted to fix for years but haven't? these questions reveal the real problems the design needs to solve. and solving real problems, rather than creating beautiful surfaces, is what produces a space that will make you happy in three years rather than just in the first month.
