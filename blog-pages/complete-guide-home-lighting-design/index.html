lighting

a complete guide to home lighting design

october 2024 · 9 min read
lighting is the element that makes or breaks every other design decision. this is everything you need to know about getting it right — room by room.

lighting is the most consequential and most consistently mishandled element of residential interior design. it is typically the last decision made in a project — after colours, materials, and furniture have been selected — and it is typically made by choosing from a catalogue rather than designing from first principles. the result is that most homes are lit the way offices are lit: uniformly, functionally, and without any relationship to the specific qualities of the spaces or the activities they support.

understanding the layers of light

effective residential lighting is always layered. the first layer is ambient light — the general illumination of a space that allows navigation and orientation. the second layer is task light — directed illumination that supports specific activities: reading, cooking, working, grooming. the third layer is accent light — illumination that directs attention to specific elements: artwork, architectural features, materials. the fourth layer is decorative light — fixtures that are visible as objects, that provide visual interest and contribute to the aesthetic character of a space. a room that has only ambient light is flat and institutional. a room with all four layers can be experienced in multiple modes and feels genuinely designed.

"lighting is not what you see. it is what you see by. get it wrong and every other design decision is compromised."

colour temperature: the invisible variable

colour temperature — measured in kelvin — is the most misunderstood variable in residential lighting. warm light (2700–3000k) renders skin tones well, makes spaces feel intimate, and is appropriate for bedrooms, living rooms, and dining areas. neutral light (3500–4000k) is appropriate for kitchens and bathrooms where accurate colour rendering is important. cool light (5000k and above) is appropriate for task-intensive workspaces but creates a clinical, uncomfortable atmosphere in living spaces. the most common error in indian residential lighting is using cool-white bulbs throughout — an understandable economy that produces consistently poor results.

room by room: what to prioritise

in the living room, the priority is adaptability — the ability to create multiple moods through dimmable circuits and layered sources. in the kitchen, the priority is task illumination — under-cabinet lighting over worktops is more important than the central overhead fixture. in the bedroom, the priorities are blackout capability during sleep and warm, low-level light in the evening that doesn't suppress melatonin. in the bathroom, the priority is vertical illumination of the face at the mirror — overhead lighting creates unflattering shadows that make grooming tasks inaccurate. in the dining area, the priority is a pendant or chandelier positioned at the correct height over the table — not recessed downlights that light the top of people's heads rather than their faces.
